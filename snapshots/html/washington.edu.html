Federal panel paints bleak picture for weather satellites

Earth-observing systems operated by the United States have entered a steep decline, imperiling the nation’s monitoring of weather, natural disasters and climate change, a report from the National Research Council warned.

Long-running and new missions are frequently delayed, lost or canceled because of budget cuts, launch failures, disorganization and changes in mission design and scope.

Faster Greenland glaciers give new insight

Changes in the speed that ice travels in more than 200 outlet glaciers indicates that Greenland's contribution to rising sea level in the 21st century might be significantly less than the upper limits some scientists thought possible.

"So far, on average we're seeing about a 30 percent speedup in 10 years," said Twila Moon, a UW doctoral student in Earth and space sciences and lead author of a paper documenting the observations.

Huskies on parade at 26th annual Windermere Cup

SEATTLE - For 26 years, the Windermere Cup has brought some of the fastest crews from around the world to the Pacific Northwest. And despite tests from all corners of the globe, Washington still manages to come out on top.

The men's and women's crew programs each won their sixth straight title, defeating international guest Argentina and a host of other visitors in dominating fashion Saturday morning on the Montlake Cut.

Handful of heavyweight trees per acre are champs

Big trees three or more feet in diameter accounted for nearly half the biomass measured at a Yosemite National Park site, yet represented only 1 percent of the trees growing there.

This means just a few towering white fir, sugar pine and incense cedars per acre at the Yosemite site are disproportionately responsible for photosynthesis, converting carbon dioxide into plant tissue and sequestering that carbon in the forest.

Cells in blood vessel cling more tightly in rapid flow

Clogging of pipes leading to the heart is the planet's number one killer. Surgeons can act as medical plumbers to repair some blockages, but we don't fully understand how this living organ deteriorates or repairs itself over time.

A new UW study could influence how doctors design drugs to treat high cholesterol, or how cardiac surgeons plan their procedures.
