    • In Memoriam

Understanding how planets form has long been a goal of astronomers. But until recently, most astronomers have been studying the formation of planets as individual, isolated cases. PSI Senior Scientist Henry Throop aims to change that, with new research into the importance of the environment surrounding young planets and stars, including interactions with other nearby planets and stars.

 

This diagram demonstrates the multi-faceted relationships between stars, planets, and the environments that they form in. As older massive stars turn into supernovas, they release new elements into the raw materials that make up new stars, in a continuing cycle of life and death. Credit: Henry Throop (PSI).

"We now know that the Sun didn't form by itself. Almost all stars form in these big groups, where you have thousands of stars all orbiting and interacting with each other,” Throop said. “And what we find is that it's a real ecosystem up there. If you want to know what kind of planets are formed, the interactions between stars are perhaps more important than the initial makeup of the stars themselves. It's nurture, not nature."

Throop was recently awarded a $280,000 grant from the NASA Astrophysics Theory Program to study this process. Throop's proposal was entitled "Bondi-Hoyle Accretion from the Interstellar Medium onto Young Protoplanetary Disks." In Throop's three-year program, he will develop new state-of-the-art computer simulations to model how giant clouds of gas and dust contribute to the makeup of young solar systems. Throop's work will build on more than a decade of research that he and his team have done in this area.
